1 · Buying strategy & timeline

Memorial Day is the right target. Lowe's runs its big appliance event May 14–27 with up to 40% off and stacking discounts (Lowe's typical "extra $50 off per $500 spent on major appliances" promo runs during this window). Best Buy, Home Depot, AJ Madison, and the major brand-direct channels all run parallel events. Black Friday and Labor Day are also strong, but Memorial Day historically has the deepest appliance-specific discounts and best inventory before the back-to-school slowdown.

Brand rebate stacking is where the real money is. Bosch's "Buy More, Save More" rebate is currently active and is the single biggest lever in this whole document — buy 2+ Bosch appliances from different categories and you get up to $2,040 back via mail-in rebate. That's why every Tier 1 recommendation below leans Bosch where the spec/reliability data supports it.

What to do this week, before sales start: (1) Confirm exact appliance dimensions are locked into the cabinet plan with your designer — fridge especially. (2) Set price-tracking alerts on AJ Madison, Best Buy, Home Depot, and Lowe's for your specific model numbers. (3) If going Bosch-heavy, download the current rebate form and read the fine print so you know what counts as "different categories."

On compromising on the in-door ice maker: Don't. Yale's 2026 data is explicit that "external ice and water dispensers create the most service calls" in the entire refrigerator category. Counter-depth tradeoff (asked about): Standard-depth 36" French doors typically run 25–27 cu. ft. vs counter-depth at 20.8–26.5 cu. ft. — roughly 15–20% less storage. Don't compromise on counter-depth for your situation — the visual and resale benefit outweighs the capacity loss.

If you want the platinum interior with wood-trim shelving specifically, the KitchenAid KRFC236SPS is the only model that has it — but you're accepting reliability risk for the aesthetic.

7 · Washer / dryer (optional this round)

Direct answer to your old-dryer question: Old conventional electric dryers use roughly 3–4 kWh per load. New conventional electric dryers use about 2–2.5 kWh. New heat-pump dryers use about 0.5–0.8 kWh — roughly 5–8x less than your existing dryer. So no, old and new dryers are not comparable on energy. If you keep the old top-load washer-and-dryer set as your "heavy duty" pair and add a new washer plus heat-pump dryer for daily use, you'll cut daily-use energy dramatically. The new washer alone idea (paired with the old dryer) loses most of the efficiency gain because the dryer is the bigger energy user in most pairs.
Critical finding on dryer venting (IRC code): You asked whether a new dryer can share the existing vent with the old one. The answer is no — International Residential Code Section M1502 explicitly states "Dryer exhaust systems shall be independent of all other systems" and shall not be connected to any other vent. Each vented dryer needs its own dedicated path to the exterior. Denver follows IRC, and an inspector will fail this on resale. So your three real options are: (1) install a second exterior vent for the new dryer, (2) make the new dryer ventless / heat-pump (no second vent needed), or (3) skip the new dryer entirely. Option 2 is almost certainly the right answer for your situation — keeps the existing vent for the old dryer, no new masonry penetration of your insulated brick walls, and far better daily-use efficiency.

Vented vs ventless tradeoffs at a glance:

Vented (conventional electric) Ventless (heat pump)
Energy per load 2–2.5 kWh 0.5–0.8 kWh
Cycle time 45–60 min 75–120 min
Heat / fabric care Hot air, can shrink delicates Low temp, gentler
Lint handling Vents lint outside Captured in trap (clean every cycle)
Install needs Vent through wall + makeup air Drain + 120V outlet only
Capacity Full size available (7+ cu. ft.) 4.0–5.3 cu. ft. typical
Service Almost any laundry tech Few techs trained on heat-pump systems
Upfront cost Lower $300–$700 more

Bottom line on the laundry question: If you're going to add anything, a stacked or all-in-one washer + heat-pump dryer is the right move because (a) it stacks for space, (b) heat-pump dryers are ventless (no need for a second exterior vent), and (c) the daily-use efficiency gain is real and substantial. The compact heat-pump units from LG and Bosch are the most reliable based on Yale's data.

If you do larger loads or back-to-back loads frequently, the WashTower (especially the heat-pump WKHC202HBA) is faster overall because you can wash and dry simultaneously. Since you're keeping the old top-loader pair as backup for big stuff, either approach works for your situation. The combo saves about 12 inches of vertical space (no stacked dryer above the controls) and is the absolute simplest install.
If keeping the old washer/dryer alongside: Plan dimensions accordingly. The LG WashTower is 27" wide × 74.4" tall. The Bosch compact is 24" wide × 67.6" stacked. The GE Profile combo is 28" wide × 46.7" tall (much shorter — cabinet space above is possible). Either works alongside the old top-loader pair if your laundry room is wide enough for both setups side by side.

On gas vs electric for the dryer

You asked for both. Honest answer: don't go gas for the new dryer. Reasons: (1) the LG WashTower's gas variant (WKGX201HBA) costs $200 more and saves you only $30–60 per year in utility costs at Denver gas/electric rates; (2) gas dryers require a gas line in the laundry room (does yours have one?); (3) if you want maximum efficiency, the heat-pump electric dryer is more efficient than any gas dryer — gas only beats conventional electric, not heat-pump electric. The decision tree collapses to electric.

9 · Methodology & sources

Reliability data. The single most useful source for this research is Yale Appliance's 2026 reliability rankings, derived from 33,000+ actual service calls completed by their Boston-area service team. This is real-world failure data, not survey responses. Their CEO Steve Sheinkopf publishes detailed methodology and updates the rankings annually. Where Yale and Consumer Reports agree, my confidence is highest. Where they disagree (e.g., LG French-doors), I noted both views.

Lab performance data. Consumer Reports' lab tests (boil time, simmer steadiness, baking evenness, temperature uniformity) and Reviewed.com's lab tests are the reference points for "does it actually work well." Both publications maintain editorial independence from manufacturers.

Owner experience. Verified-purchase reviews from Best Buy and Home Depot, plus Houzz, Bogleheads, and Greenbuildingadvisor forum threads — used selectively, weighted toward owners who have had products 1+ years and report specific failure modes or longevity outcomes.

Reddit, transparently: Reddit threads (r/appliances, r/AskCulinary, r/HomeImprovement) were not included in this research. The web search tool used here returns curated results that don't surface Reddit content reliably. Steve Sheinkopf at Yale Appliance is frequently cited as the gold-standard source on appliance reliability across Reddit threads, which validates the primary source used here, but you're not getting the unfiltered "I've owned this for 3 years and here's what broke" Reddit perspective in this document. If you want to cross-check before buying, search the specific model number on Reddit for the recent owner experience.

Pricing. Current MSRP from manufacturer sites; current retail from AJ Madison, Best Buy, Lowe's, Plesser's, and Gerhard's; "target prices" derived from observed sale-price patterns over the past 12 months on these retailers, plus active rebate programs.

What I didn't include and why. Speed Queen washer/dryer (excellent reliability but no truly stackable option that meets your needs); Miele compact units (highest-end reliability but $1,500+ more than Bosch for similar capability, plus 15–20% price increase announced for 2026 due to tariffs); Wolf / Sub-Zero / Thermador / SKS (luxury tier, not requested); Samsung refrigerators and laundry (Yale data: weakest service infrastructure of major brands, repeated owner reports of slow service); Fisher & Paykel 36" French-door (they only make a 32" counter-depth that would force a cabinet redesign, and Denver service availability is thin).

Reminders for the actual buying day

1. Confirm rough openings with cabinet plan one final time before purchase — fridge and range cutout dimensions especially. Returns are expensive on appliances. 2. Schedule delivery for after construction is substantially complete. Ask the retailer to hold-for-delivery (Lowe's and AJ Madison both do this routinely; Home Depot is hit-or-miss). 3. Register every appliance for warranty within 30 days. The Bosch and LG warranties require registration. 4. Save all receipts and rebate forms in one folder. The Bosch rebate is mail-in and has a deadline. 5. Don't accept the dealer-installed protection plan upsell. It rarely pays out and AppliancesConnection / extended warranties through credit cards are usually better value if you want one.
